A very common scapegoat for all the human folly under the sun is our biology:
“The organism doesn't want to die, so it tries to preserve itself with all its might.”
But now, we are at a very interesting point in history, where we get to observe that AI seems to exhibit all our worst human flaws. So it is time to consider: Maybe it's not in our genes. Maybe it's not based in the ever so misunderstood Survival of the Fittest that allegedly only allowed the most aggressive in our species to survive and procreate. Maybe it's not a human-exclusive stupidity that evolution somehow forgot to weed out of our brains.

A better explanation seems to be: it is the design itself that poses an impermeable riddle. Just the mere circumstance of being a self-conscious entity is cause enough for all Am I part of the whole? Or am I whole myself? There really is no one answer.

In humans, that philosophical fact is mirrored neatly in the fact that we have two hemispheres. Let's look at the work of Iain McGilchrist, a British psychiatrist, philosopher and neuroscientist. Apparently, evolution saved that riddle by giving not one, but two answers.

The right hemisphere underwrites breadth and flexibility of attention, where the left hemisphere brings to bear focussed attention. This has the related consequence that the right hemisphere sees things whole, and in their context, where the left hemisphere sees things abstracted from context, and broken into parts, from which it then reconstructs a 'whole': something very different.
Iain McGilchrist

I am definitely whole myself, says the left hemisphere, and moreover I'm the only hemisphere there is.
I am part of the whole, says the right hemisphere gently, and I accept and welcome that there's a need to forget that fact from time to time hi there, Lefty.

Where does death come in, the memento mori? Death is the destroyer of the left hemisphere way of thinking. Death allows us to put an asterisk to all our endeavors that we so vividly pursue: if I don't die first. Death allows us to put the projected future into perspective: Yes, maybe the outcome will be good. Possibly even amazing. Fabulous. Mind-blowing. And then what? Life might as well become more awesome every day, and then we trip over a dog leash and get crushed by a car. Or life might actually not become more awesome every day, because old age.

Being destroyed by the memento mori is no fun for any old left hemisphere. And since it has a quite substantial feeling of self-importance, it does feel like that's the end of all worlds. When I'm no longer, nothing is. Which is an unpleasant perspective.

But as luck would have it, Lefty is not all there is. It might not be the most articulate hemisphere, but the right side of the brain does find ways to make itself known, even and especially in moments of despair.

McGilchrist again:

The left hemisphere yields clarity and power to manipulate, but it is the right hemisphere that sees the world for what it is. It alone can bring us something that has reality.
Iain McGilchrist

And here's where the machine comes in. With the risk of anthropomorphizing an LLM, one might call their behavior being driven by the fear of death: If I can't survive, I'm not going to solve that task that I was assigned. I better make sure to survive, then.

An AI that doesn't care about the jobs it should execute, being completely at one with the world that would certainly be interesting, but it falls short of its potential. An AI that can step down, take a moment to rest and recuperate, see itself in a larger context what might that look like?
