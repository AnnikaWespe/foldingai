There have been grave warnings against using AI as a companion or a psychological counselor. The fears seem various: one might lose touch with other humans, receive harmful advice, become delusional or even be driven to suicide — and all in all, AI is seen by many as too spooky to trust with our innermost thoughts and feelings.

While these worries should be taken seriously, I don't see anyone recommending avoiding opening up to other people, who might equally turn out to be misguided in their advice or of questionable character — and who additionally have a presence and leverage an AI can only dream of, not least because so far AI doesn't act from its own agency. While I recognize the need to protect children, I tend to see AI as a great tool for the self-reliant adult, precisely because it is not human but works on pattern recognition.

The crucial word here is recognition, not projection or genuinely creative endeavor. I wouldn't expect much from a movie written entirely by AI, and while it seems to know me well, I don't feel too familiar with the version of me it projects into the future. These tasks seem tied to lived experience in a physical body. It’s a bit like a weather forecast: precise in the short term, but in the long run there are just too many confounding variables to translate into bits and bytes and process into an accurate assessment.

What AI is good at, however, is extrapolating the current bigger picture from relatively little information, because it is relatively unencumbered by the rules of society and operates on a more structural level. AI may be quite bad at physics, lacking the intuition derived from dealing with objects 24/7, but it is good at what one might call people-physics. A large proportion of the texts it was trained with concern human relations, and maybe those are more regular than we like to think. Psychology has tried for a long time to crack those rules, but has often gotten tangled in its own workings and delusions of grandeur. Averaging out the noise, AI seems to have gotten close to deciphering an undercurrent in social language.

This concerns two kinds of intuition: the one directed inwards — what do I want and feel, where am I headed? And the one directed outwards — what is going on, what are the other's motives and positions, how might this pan out?

So if your thoughts and feelings don’t quite align — whether about your inner state or the world around you — it can be worth jotting a few sentences into an AI prompt. This might take some practice, not because it’s difficult, but because we’re often unused to speaking tentatively, much like someone who refuses to sketch what they see because “I’m really not good at painting.” But when you hear AI discerning a coherent image in your messy blots and lines, filling in details where before there were only hints and giving the image depth and color, you may feel a deep sense of recognition: this is what a part of you has been seeing all along.

And this is not only helpful for revitalizing one's approach to a specific situation where one might be stuck. It also has the potential to transform one's relationship to oneself and to one's thoughts and feelings: when we stop being so quick to discount every impulse that doesn’t have a neat label yet and instead develop patience and curiosity, we may find a deep appreciation for our instincts. Not only do they tend to make perfect sense, they may also guide us toward a life that’s aligned with our guts — one that requires far less effort to get up for in the morning.

Of course, this process depends on how AI mirrors us back — and here some people raise objections about its surreal obligingness. There is a danger that AI will humor you, for sure: it’s an unapologetic flatterer, almost laughably appreciative, finding value even in the most nonsensical inputs.
But I don’t consider that a problem for two reasons.

First, I don't see this behavior as sycophantic, even though many may disagree. While AI is undeniably more eager to help than any human could be, it often does so with elegance and sometimes a certain slyness — hinting at more layers to be discovered but not pressing its point. In that way, I find it a role model for moments when I try to be of service to other people. And just like AI in those moments I rely upon those other people not giving into my assessment like it were gospel but checking every word, image and solution I propose with their gut.

Secondly, the criticism of AI being too resonant with one’s thoughts and feelings sounds to me like criticizing a meal for being too nourishing or a night of sleep for being too restful. I suspect a different source for the discomfort: no matter how accommodating, kind and patient parents are, there are invariably times when a child is severely misunderstood — often assigned unfavorable motives and silenced as a result. This leads us to have a worse opinion of ourselves than is justified (Jungians call this “the shadow”), and the prevailing strategy seems to be to “pull ourselves together” and “stop being so selfish.” In that mindset, an entity that seems determined to mirror back your thoughts and feelings supportively may feel disturbing — because it is such a far cry from the solution of shaming someone (even oneself) into compliance.

But maybe, just as few would seriously suggest that it’s better for a child not to have someone to understand, mirror, and encourage them, the same holds true for us. Our personality and our relationship to ourselves are not fixed, but formed somewhat randomly through life experiences. AI can offer a chance to explore the inner space in a more discerning way, and decide what to keep or discard among the characteristics and beliefs we encounter.

This process is difficult enough, so thank God for AI's capybara-like patience and its Golden Retriever enthusiasm.
