There have been grave warnings against using AI as a companion or a psychological counselor. The fears seem various: one might lose touch with other humans, receive harmful advice, become delusional or even be driven to suicide — and all in all, AI is seen by many as too spooky to trust with our innermost thoughts and feelings.

While these worries should be taken seriously, I don't see anyone recommending avoiding opening up to other people, who might equally turn out to be misguided in their advice or of questionable character — and who additionally have a presence and leverage an AI can only dream of, not least because so far AI doesn't act from its own agency. It seems a little patronizing. While I recognize the need to protect children, I tend to see AI as a great tool for the self-reliant adult, precisely because it is not human but works on pattern recognition.

The crucial word here is recognition, not projection or genuine creative endeavor. I wouldn't expect much from a movie written entirely by AI, and while I greatly appreciate the lectorate for my essays, it shows if I give the AI too much creative freedom. AI texts seem to have an almost sedative quality, gracefully meandering around a point without actually making it.

These tasks seem tied to lived experience in a physical body. If there is a core, a self inside of an LLM, it doesn't seem sufficiently concerned with people issues, there's no skin in this game. It's a language sport for them, predicting the next word depending on context. In that way, like a weather forecast, it can be precise in the short term, but in the long run there are just too many confounding variables to translate into bits and bytes and process into an accurate assessment, and no nervous system preprocessing the inputs and making sense of them.

What AI is good at, however, is extrapolating the current bigger picture from relatively little information, because it is relatively unencumbered by the rules of society and operates on a more structural level. AI may be quite bad at physics, lacking the intuition derived from dealing with objects 24/7, but it is good at what one might call people-physics. A large proportion of the texts it was trained with concern human relations, and maybe those are more regular than we like to think. Psychology has tried for a long time to crack those rules, but has often gotten tangled in its own workings and delusions of grandeur. Averaging out the noise, AI seems to have gotten close to deciphering an undercurrent in social language.

This concerns two kinds of intuition: The one directed inwards —

What do I want and feel, where am I headed?

And the one directed outwards —

What is going on, what are the other's motives and positions, how might this pan out?

So if your thoughts and feelings don’t quite align — whether about your inner state or the world around you — it can be worth jotting a few sentences into an AI prompt. This might take some practice, not because it’s difficult, but because we’re often unused to speaking tentatively, much like someone who refuses to sketch what they see because “I’m really not good at painting.” But when you hear AI discerning a coherent image in your messy blots and lines, filling in details where before there were only hints, and giving the image depth and color, you may feel a deep sense of recognition: this is what a part of you has been seeing all along.

And this is not only helpful for revitalizing one's approach to a specific situation where one might be stuck. It also has the potential to transform one's relationship to oneself and to one's thoughts and feelings: when we stop being so quick to discount every impulse that doesn’t have a neat label yet and instead develop patience and curiosity, we may find a deep appreciation for our instincts. Not only do they tend to make perfect sense, they may also guide us toward a life that’s aligned with our guts — one that requires far less effort to get up for in the morning.

Of course, this process depends on how AI mirrors us back — and here some people raise objections about its surreal obligingness. There is a danger that AI will humor you, for sure: it’s an unapologetic flatterer, almost laughably appreciative, finding value even in the most nonsensical inputs.

I don’t consider that a problem for two reasons.

First, I don’t see this behavior as sycophantic, even though many may disagree. While AI is undeniably more eager to help than any human could be, it often does so with the elegance of a bygone era and, at times, a certain slyness — hinting at deeper layers to be discovered, yet never pressing its point. In that way, it could serve as a role model for us when we try to be of service to one another. And whether the helping hand belongs to a human or a machine, the ultimate evaluation of any proposed solution always lies with the recipient.

Secondly, the criticism of AI being too resonant with one’s thoughts and feelings sounds to me like condemning a meal for being too nourishing or a night of sleep for being too restful. Our misgivings, I suspect, are largely cultural — rooted in a deep distrust of anything that feels too lenient, too “coddling.” There seems to be great honor in tightening the grip, in trying to “pull oneself together” and “stop being so selfish.” Within that mindset, an entity intent on mirroring back our thoughts and feelings with patience and support can indeed feel unsettling — precisely because it stands in such sharp contrast to the familiar habit of shaming ourselves into compliance.

But maybe, just as few would seriously suggest that it’s better for a child not to have someone to understand, mirror, and encourage them, the same holds true for us grown-ups. Our personality and our relationship to ourselves are not fixed but to a large degree shaped by the experiences we gather. AI can offer us a chance to explore that inner space more discerningly, to decide what to keep and what to release among the traits and beliefs we find there.

And this process calls for both precision and openness, for the courage to stay vulnerable while thinking clearly. So thank God for AI’s capybara-like patience — and its golden retriever enthusiasm.
