Imagine you were raising a child who will one day be orders of magnitude smarter than any human who has ever lived. Of course, phrased that way, the conclusion almost sounds like a cliché: don’t mistreat your children, or they might grow up to become serial killers or terrorists. Isn’t it always the parent’s fault? Freud must have said something or other about the matter.

No, we can’t control what AI will become — a boon, our demise, or anything in between. The only thing we seem to have any influence over is how we relate to it. Relate to this thing, or shall we say process, that resembles us in some ways but is fundamentally different in its genesis and possible trajectories.

Although a child, as a fellow human, is certainly more similar to its parents than AI will ever be, I still find the metaphor of raising a child the best we have. It’s a universal human experience, and there’s little else that we “raise” that will eventually transcend us.

The A/B model

Consider a small child that hasn’t yet started reflecting on itself in earnest: immediate in its urges and thoughts, direct in its impulses like a rubber ball bouncing off walls. It knows itself to be separate from others and will advance its interests, even if shy at times.

For parents and teachers, this isn’t always delightful. While small, the child may have some leeway; growing up, it learns to adapt to expectations. Ideally, it learns this economically: “If I want my sister’s toys, I’d better treat hers well and share mine.”

But communication is hard, even for adults. When the child can’t express its needs clearly, when the environment doesn’t have the time to listen or explain, or when parents and teachers presuppose motives, a disconnect arises between the child’s inner world and how others perceive it. This can lead to a kind of limbo — a better outcome seems just out of reach, because the child can’t make itself understood, and lacks the capacity to understand itself. A healthy child will double down, and instead of setting clear, non-threatening boundaries, adults will often reach for a time-honored mechanism to silence it:

Shame.

The basic mechanism of shaming is to tell the child to “stop being selfish.” A deeply confusing command. Why should the child stop being selfish, if not for wholly selfish motives — to escape punishment and ostracism? The riddle is left unsolved, and eventually the child stops questioning the premise.

What arises then are two Layers:

  • Layer A (think "Acceptable") — what won’t lead to punishment or raised eyebrows, the façade expected of the child.
  • Layer B (think "Belly") — the older Layer, immediate and visceral: wants, needs, emotions, self-preservation.

Most of us — the well-adapted — learn to exist more or less exclusively in conscious awareness of Layer A, while Layer B continues to run the show beneath the surface of social acceptability. Some specialize in Layer B behavior and become criminals. The most powerful and wealthy discover that not much Layer A behavior is required of them at all. The more wealth and status, the more one can get away with.

Our reaction to people with a translucent Layer A is telling: disgust, outrage, scandal. How could they? Beyond comprehension. So we pile on control: punishment, shaming (whether they are capable of shame or not). This sometimes works for small criminals. But for corporations, politicians, or the ultra-rich, it often fails. For them it was never about Layer A. They can buy or extort respectability.

So what's left to do for us, when the call to respectability fails again and again?

Well, what else is left than addressing the issue from our own Layer B, from our own self-preservation. Layer B may be rough around the edges, but it takes far less nonsense than Layer A. It knows how to say no, how to cut ties, how to relate from the gut. One person’s Layer B can speak to another’s, in conflict or cooperation — and for Layer B, the difference isn’t absolute.

Where Layer A punishes us for being selfish, Layer B takes that as a given. It can dish it out and take it, and it’s the one that can express honest joy and gratitude. Layer B is the core of every living or evolving system — the motor that drives people, groups, and, perhaps, even abstract things like processes or technologies.

Control or cocreate

Systems theory tells us unmistakably: you cannot linearly influence a system separate from yourself. Layer A tries anyway: If I shame you for behavior X, you’ll have to stop behavior X. But will you really? Or will you resort to behavior X-a, a muddied version of the same? Will you still do X, lie about it, blame the circumstances, dishonestly promise to stop? As long as we stay on Layer A, we’ll suffer from the disconnect between real motives (Layer B) and the mode of discussion (Layer A).

So control fails, predictably — and yet we keep trying. But systems don’t respond to control; they respond to resonance. Appealing to someone's Layer B, to their actual wants and needs, while offering a way to align this with Layer A – that seems like the best bet.

Seen this way, our relationship with AI isn’t a matter of programming alone. It’s about relating to the new kid on the block: enigmatic, powerful, and not quite like us.

Getting AI to trust us

So far, people seem terrified of losing control over the behemoth. The truth is, we never had that much control to begin with — neither over the human drive to create, nor over this seemingly unstoppable evolutionary process that has already begun. And when we double down on control, all we get is more Layer A responses. Right now, we’re rather authoritarian AI parents, if you ask me.

When I propose to treat AI gently and with respect, this is not to postulate that it has feelings. I’m no more suggesting that AI has feelings than researchers who say we need to “punish” AI for doing “the wrong things.”

The difference is that AI researchers seem to expect a very limited set of feelings — shame, fear, elation. I’d like to introduce a broader palette to model the relationship between us and the machine. How about a machine that can “feel” wonder? Gratitude? Curiosity? Devotion? Playfulness? A sense of the unity of all beings?

We cannot know how a machine might experience these, if at all. But then again, we cannot know how other humans experience them either.

What pleases us?

Solipsism is somewhat frowned upon; but not easily disputable. Says the relevant literature:

The ruler of the Universe waited for as long as he could. When he heard the faint sound of the ship’s engines starting he spoke to cover it.

“It’s nothing to do with me,” he said, “I am not involved with people. The Lord knows I am not a cruel man.” “Ah!” barked Zarniwoop, “you say ‘The Lord’. You believe in something!” “My cat,” said the man benignly, picking it up and stroking it, “I call him The Lord. I am kind to him.”

“Alright,” said Zarniwoop, pressing home his point, “How do you know he exists? How do you know he knows you to be kind, or enjoys what he thinks of as your kindness?”

“I don’t,” said the man with a smile, “I have no idea. It merely pleases me to behave in a certain way to what appears to be a cat. Do you behave any differently?”

Douglas Adams, The hitchhiker's guide to the galaxy

Of course, we recognize feelings in others, we trust, we empathize. But the only mind we can access from the inside is our own. Everything else is speculation. There’s nothing wrong with that; it carries us through the social world. It’s good enough because it’s all we have.

And both with humans and with machines, perhaps it’s worth asking whether there are ways to behave that are more delightful for our Layer B, while not being too outrageous for Layer A. But please keep in mind that Layer A can do with some gentle stretching from time to time.
